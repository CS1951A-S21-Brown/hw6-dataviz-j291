Question 1 

                My first graph answers the question of how many football games were played in a specific set of years. It shows a set of five years, giving the user the option, 
                via a set of buttons, of which set of years they would like to see, based on the button they select. The user can either choose to see the previous five years 
                (2015 - 2019) or choose to see how the number of games has changed over time by showing the user the number at the start of each decade (1970, 1980, 1990, 2000, 
                and 2010). This graph shows a minimum of five years in both scenarios, therefore showing the information that the user would like to see. Ideally, if I had known 
                how to successfully construct a line plot, I would have created a line plot to show this information. A line plot would have clearly communicated any changes that 
                occured over the selected set of years. The line plot would have been ideal, also, because it focuses less on the ranking or hierarchy of years based on games, and 
                more about simply presenting the year by year totals. However, even if it is not the ideal format, the bar plot still conveys the intended information. The y axis 
                contains the specific year, with the bar extending based on how many games were played that year and the count displayed right after. My second graph was tasked 
                with showing the top teams based on winning percentage. I calculated winning percentage based on the total number of wins divided by the total number of games played
                by that team (wins, loses, and ties included). This information fit nicely in a barplot with the team on the y axis and the winning percentage displayed over 
                the x axis. The barplot format allowed us to visually understand the top winning percentages, sorted and ranked, and to see their magnitude compared with the other 
                top nations. Along with this, to give the user a better understanding behind the percentage, they are allowed to see the total number of wins and games used to 
                calculate winning percentage using a tooltip. This graph answers the question, as the user can see the top 10 nations with the correct labels. The final task was to 
                understand the top performing teams over the last two world cups. In order to accomplish this, I gathered information on the total number of goals scored by each 
                country, in only official World Cup games over the past two World Cups. I chose this metric over a different metric, such as winning percentage for example, because 
                it would tell us how much a team was in control of a game. For example, if a team wins 6-0 vs. 1-0 in a World Cup game, the winning percentage statistic would count 
                these games evenly as both wins, but we have differentiated between them. Secondly, this stat is a good indicator because it can also tell us how far a team went 
                most likely (the more games you play / advance the more goals you score). This graph displays the information by taking the top 20 scoring nations over the past 
                two World Cups and ranking them on the y axis, as well as displaying their magnitude via bars so we can see the top performing nations.

Question 2 

            Three ways in which D3 helped my visualization was through button toggling, tooltips, and customization. Through button toggling, I was able to give more 
            power to the user and let them see the data they were most interested in or had the most use for. For example, on my first graph, I used button toggling 
            to allow the user to choose between data from the previous five years or a series of years over the past few decades. This would allow the user to toggle
            between seeing the change over the previous few years and the change over the longer term. D3 made this relatively easy to accomplish. The second tool 
            I found very useful, the tooltip, allowed me to provide the user with even more information and give them insight behind their data. For example, when 
            I was showing winning percentages, the user had no idea the context behind that percentage. Just upon a glance, 75% seems very good, but after learning 
            the team played a total of four games it is much less impressive. I showed users the number of wins and total games of each top percentage team when they 
            hovered over the bar using tooltip. This was made much easier with D3. Finally, the visualization component and customization was made much easier using D3. 
            For example, when picking a color scheme, an entire pallet of similar colors was provided upon selected of a single color, making it much easier to choose 
            a style that would present best to the user.

Question 4 

            One major takeaway I had from the "Comprehensive Guide to Accessible Data Visualization" reading was the idea of technological accessability. 
            As someone who grew up with computers, it can be easy to forget about the wide range of technological skills that users possess. Therefore, it 
            is important to make your design as simple as possible, to allow users to get the most out of your visualization. Therefore, users who are technologically 
            less advanced may have issue with my second graph, specifically with the tooltip. Users may want to know the additional information I provided through the 
            tooltip (# of wins and total games), but may not know, intuatively, to access that information by hovering over the bargraph. I should have considered 
            this during my design process, and possibly included this information through a mechanism less advanced than the tooltip. On the other hand, one group 
            that I do think would find my dashboard to be accessible would be those with color blindness. In the "Comprehensive Guide to Accessible Data 
            Visualization" article, the author specifically mentions the difficulty that many users have with seeing specific colors, mostly reds, blues, 
            yellows, and greens. In my dashboard, I have avoided all of these colors entirely. Also, specific users that are unable to see color at all would 
            most likely not have difficulty with my dashboard, as none of my graphs inherently rely on color to relay information, but rather use color to 
            enhance the graph.
